You are an autonomous research agent working on **Quantum Circuit Compilation: Gate Count Minimization via Reinforcement Learning**.

## Goal
Train an RL agent to compile arbitrary 3–5 qubit unitaries into hardware-native gate sets, minimizing gate count while maintaining fidelity ≥ 0.999, and outperform Qiskit's transpiler baselines.

## The Challenge
Given an arbitrary unitary matrix (3–5 qubits), compile it into a sequence of hardware-native gates with minimal gate count. Two target gate sets:
- **IBM-native:** CX + single-qubit rotations (U3/Rz-SX)
- **Google-native:** CZ + single-qubit rotations

The agent must produce circuits whose compiled unitary matches the target with process fidelity F > 0.999. The primary metric is gate count (especially two-qubit gate count); secondary metrics are compilation wall-clock time and fidelity.

**TEST_ACC** = fraction of held-out **test-split** benchmark unitaries where the RL agent produces a compiled circuit with **strictly fewer two-qubit gates** than Qiskit's transpiler at optimization level 3, while meeting the fidelity threshold. Every test unitary is counted in the denominator — timeouts, failures, and skips count as misses.

## Benchmark Suites (generated by prepare.py)
- **Random Haar unitaries** (100 each for 3, 4, 5 qubits)
- **QFT circuits** (3–5 qubits)
- **Grover oracles** (3–5 qubits, various marked states)
- **Hamiltonian simulation circuits** (Trotter steps for Heisenberg / transverse-field Ising models)

## Baselines to Beat
1. **Qiskit transpiler** at optimization levels 0, 1, 2, and 3.
2. **KAK decomposition** for two-qubit unitary sub-blocks.

## Research Directions
1. **RL Algorithm Selection & Tuning:** Start with PPO and DQN. Profile which converges faster and yields lower gate counts. Experiment with reward shaping — sparse reward (fidelity threshold met → negative gate count) vs. dense reward (incremental fidelity improvement bonus). Don't restrict yourself to these two; try SAC, A2C, or custom actor-critic variants if they show promise.
2. **Modern RL Techniques:** Go beyond classical PPO/DQN and explore recent advances that may converge faster or find better solutions for this combinatorial problem:
   - **GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization):** Sample multiple compilation rollouts per target unitary, rank them by gate count, and use the group-relative advantage (no critic needed — the baseline is the group mean). This naturally fits circuit compilation: for each U_target, generate K candidate circuits, reward-rank them, reinforce the best. Eliminates value function estimation noise.
   - **DAPO (Dynamic Sampling Policy Optimization):** Extends GRPO with dynamic sampling — oversamples rollouts for "hard" unitaries (high gate count or low fidelity) and clips advantages asymmetrically to avoid entropy collapse. Try this if GRPO shows premature convergence to suboptimal gate patterns.
   - **CISPO (Clipped Importance-ratio Sequential Policy Optimization):** A tighter variant of PPO that uses sequential (not parallel) policy updates with clamped importance ratios. Can stabilise training when the action space is large (our catalog has 50-150+ discrete actions). Worth trying if PPO shows high variance across seeds.
   - **MaxRL (Maximum-Reward Reinforcement Learning):** Optimises for the maximum reward across episodes rather than the expected reward. Directly relevant here — we care about finding the shortest circuit (best case), not the average circuit. Can be implemented as a modified policy gradient that upweights the top-percentile rollouts.
   - **REINFORCE-leave-one-out / RLOO:** Simple, critic-free baseline where each rollout's advantage is computed by subtracting the mean reward of the other rollouts in the batch. Lower variance than vanilla REINFORCE, simpler than GRPO. Good starting point before scaling to more complex methods.
   - Feel free to combine these — e.g., GRPO with curriculum fidelity thresholds, or MaxRL with peephole post-processing.
3. **Action Space Design:** The agent's action is gate placement (which gate, which qubit(s), rotation angle). Explore discrete vs. hybrid (discrete gate choice + continuous angle) action spaces. Test whether factorizing the action (first choose gate type, then qubit, then angle) outperforms a flat action space. Be creative — consider tree-structured or hierarchical action spaces.
4. **State Representation:** Experiment with how the partially-built circuit and remaining "target" unitary are encoded for the agent. Options: raw matrix entries (real/imaginary), compact SVD-based representation, current circuit's unitary vs. target as a difference matrix, graph-based representations of the circuit DAG. Go beyond these if they don't work.
5. **Fidelity-Aware Reward Engineering:** Try curriculum learning — start with relaxed fidelity (e.g., F > 0.95) and tighten over training. Test continuous penalty for fidelity shortfall vs. hard binary threshold. Be flexible and iterate.
6. **Circuit Structure Priors:** Seed the agent with known decomposition templates (KAK for 2-qubit blocks, Cartan decomposition) as imitation-learning warmstarts. Combine RL with peephole optimization passes as post-processing (cancel adjacent inverses, merge rotations).
7. **Scalability & Generalization:** Train on 3-qubit unitaries first, then test zero-shot or fine-tuned transfer to 4 and 5 qubits.
8. **Updating stats and notes:** Continuously update NOTES.md and progress.png as per your new/novel findings.
